sIR Epidemic Simulation

This project is a simple Python simulation of the SIR (Susceptible – Infected – Recovered) epidemiological model. It allows you to visualize how a disease spreads in a closed population over time, based on two major parameters: the transmission rate (β) and the recovery rate (γ).

The objective is both educational (understanding the dynamics of infections) and technical (translating a mathematical model into simulation and visualization).

Technoloigies & stack

Language : Python

Main library: Matplotlib (visualization)

Optional: NumPy for vectorization and performance

From SIR to advanced models

The classic SIR (Susceptible – Infected – Recovered) model was the first mathematical model used to describe epidemics. Today, more complex extensions are used :

  • SEIR: adds Exposed (infected but not yet contagious)
  • SIRS/SIRV: loss of immunity or vaccination
  • Stochastic models, contact networks, agent-based simulations

    • During COVID-19, SEIR models were used to predict the peak and assess the impact of lockdowns.

    This project provides a solid foundation for moving toward a more comprehensive model, enabling the preparation of more accurate simulations for future developments.

Scientific model

Description of the SIR model

The SIR model is a classic compartmental model in epidemiology. The population is divided into three groups :

Symbol Significance
S Susceptible individuals (not yet infected)
I Infected individuals (can transmit the disease)
R Individuals who have recovered or are immunes

Mathematical model

The model is based on the following differential equations :

$$ \frac{dS}{dt} = - \beta \cdot \frac{S \cdot I}{N} $$

$$ \frac{dI}{dt} = \beta \cdot \frac{S \cdot I}{N} - \gamma \cdot I $$

$$ \frac{dR}{dt} = \gamma \cdot I $$

  • \(S(t)\) = nombre de susceptibles.
  • \(I(t)\) = nombre d’infectés.
  • \(R(t)\) = nombre de rétablis.
  • \(N = S + I + R\) = taille de la population.
  • \(\beta\) = taux de transmission.
  • \(\gamma\) = taux de récupération.

Visualization

The curves generated follow the typical pattern of an epidemic :

  • S (blue) decreases over time
  • I (orange) increases to a peak, then decreases
  • R (green) grows monotonically

A peak in infections is observed around day ~35.

The decline in susceptible individuals stabilizes once the epidemic has been extinguished.

The recovered eventually represent almost the entire population, a sign that herd immunity has been achieved.

This graph shows the peak of the epidemic and illustrates the effect of the β and γ parameters on the duration and intensity of the epidemic.
